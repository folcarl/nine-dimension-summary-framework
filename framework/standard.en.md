# Nine-Dimension Summary Standard

This standard guides summaries of intellectual, cultural, analytical, journalistic, artistic, and research material: transcripts, interviews, lectures, essays, books, podcasts, documentaries, commentary, video-derived text, and reports.

The nine dimensions are not a rigid template. They are a set of questions every strong summary must survive. A good summary should not visibly stuff nine headings into the output. It should read like a clear reconstructed essay whose judgments have passed the nine-dimensional test.

## Core Principle

A source is never just "content." It exists in at least three time relations:

- the time and environment in which it was produced;
- the time, world, or reality it represents;
- the later moment from which readers or viewers interpret it.

It is also not only "what was said." A source includes the form, medium, rhetoric, evidence, emotional posture, audience expectation, and deeper problem being processed.

The task of summary is therefore not compression. It is reconstruction of meaning: what the material says, why it says it this way, why it matters now, what it clarifies, and what it obscures.

## The Nine Questions

1. **Production time**: In what historical, technical, platform, institutional, or political environment was the source produced?
2. **Represented time**: What period, reality, memory, present problem, or imagined future does the source present?
3. **Reading/viewing time**: Why does this source matter to readers or viewers now?
4. **Surface subject**: What does the source explicitly discuss? Who, what, where, when, and through what argument?
5. **Form and medium**: What form organizes the content: interview, lecture, report, commentary, documentary, memoir, data analysis, or another form?
6. **Deeper problem**: What is the source really processing beneath the surface subject?
7. **Perceived object**: What do audiences think they are consuming: knowledge, inside information, entertainment, history, investment reference, political judgment, or emotional release?
8. **Viewing position**: Through what entry point, stance, bias, anxiety, or desire does the audience approach the material?
9. **Effect of viewing**: What does the act of reading or watching satisfy, clarify, distort, or replace?

## Execution Modes

Use **full mode** for long interviews, dense lectures, political or historical material, economic or investment material, research reports, complex documentaries, major public events, long podcasts, batch work, or whenever the material is ambiguous.

Use **lightweight mode** only for short, low-density, low-complexity material. Lightweight mode may compress sections, but it still must cover: core content, the real problem, form/viewing risk, at least one concrete limitation or evidence gap, and overall evaluation with scores.

For batch work, do not lower the standard just because there are many files. Each source must be summarized independently before any cross-source synthesis.

## Required Functional Modules

Every full summary must cover these functions, even if section titles are adapted:

1. **Core content / core argument**: restore the main line before evaluating it.
2. **The real problem it handles**: identify the deeper issue beneath the surface topic.
3. **Form and viewing logic**: analyze medium, credibility mechanism, audience expectation, and viewing risk.
4. **Evidence chain and reasoning**: explain how the source moves from evidence to claims.
5. **Critique and limits**: identify logic errors, evidence gaps, alternative positions, and framework risks.
6. **External verification boundary**: distinguish what the source says from what still needs checking.
7. **Implications for judgment and action**: explain what the material helps readers judge, and where it may mislead them.
8. **Follow-up observation list**: for analytical, economic, investment, policy, or evolving topics, list concrete data, events, or signals to watch.
9. **Overall evaluation and scores**: give a short overall judgment and a scored table.

## Writing Style

Write like a serious human explaining a difficult source. Avoid empty phrases such as "important significance," "worth attention," or "needs vigilance" unless you specify exactly what matters. Do not overuse tables. Do not turn the method into visible performance. A reader should feel the source has been processed through time, form, evidence, audience, and limits without necessarily seeing the phrase "nine dimensions."

## Fact And Judgment Boundaries

Separate four things:

- what the source explicitly says;
- what the source implies;
- what the summarizer infers;
- what still needs external verification.

Useful wording includes:

- "The source argues..."
- "The interviewee claims..."
- "The transcript suggests..."
- "From the material alone, we can only infer..."
- "This still requires external verification..."

Do not treat a compelling narrative as proof. Do not treat a familiar viewpoint as evidence.

## Economic And Investment Material

For macro, market, industry, company, asset-price, policy, trade, AI capex, employment, consumption, or real-estate material, include:

- core variables;
- transmission mechanisms;
- conditions under which the argument holds or fails;
- indicators to monitor;
- risks and counterexamples;
- implications for judgment, research, or decision boundaries.

Do not present the output as investment advice or tell the user what to buy or sell.
